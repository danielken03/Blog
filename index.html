The Introduction

Friday, April 3rd 2026

This is a new experience for me,

I thought I might want to try blogging. I have realised I really like talking to people about a variety of topics, especially those that I am very interested and have a passion for. But who am I? Who is Daniel Kennedy? What reason do you have to listen to me?

I am 22 years old, I work in the hospitality industry, and I have a lot of hobbies I invest my time into. Over the years I have been pretty devoted in juggling, even attempting to make it my career, rock climbing, audio visual work and video editing, and much more. Now my hobbies include golf in my spare time when weather is nice and computer programming.
I started programming like a lot of people with Minecraft, the game that inspires people to be creative and make things! While Minecraft is a lot of fighting zombies and building a house, to me it was a gateway drug that lead to more than I would have dreamed. I always liked building, when I was in high school my family was building a new house IRL, so I took the blueprints and built the house to 3:1 scale in Minecraft so we could see what it would look like from a first person perspective. This was always fun to be, but what I really enjoyed was the feeling of control in how the game functioned with the various commands and customisability. I created a dungeon map with custom mobs with different abilities and designed the corridors to look like you were walking through the catacombs in France. I felt like a true game developer, I felt like I was just given the tools to create my own game, even though I was just using the features given by Mojang. I started playing around 14 years old; my brain works very logically and this really got my gears turning. I loved the feeling of logically thinking through how to tackle an idea with the limitations of Minecraft. This started my passion for creativity and art, which leads to where we are today.

I've never been a good artist,

I took art classes in high school and tried my hand at drawing, painting, wood carving, photography and videography, but nothing really clicked for me. I was always disappointed with the outcome of my work, especially with painting. Geez Louise I just couldn't understand how paintings can look so real! I am always left with blobs of color no matter how hard I try. The only good painting I had was a paint by number! I have a knack for creativity, but I could never find an outlet that suited me, until I started programming. I finally found something that rewards a lot of time and dedication and book smarts with accomplishment and a tangible product that I can be proud of.

I tend to pride myself on my knowledge, and I try to be as knowledgeable as I can. I'm always listening to podcasts or watching YouTube videos to learn new things, and at work I am very dedicated to learning new things and being the best I can. This definitely has its downsides that I will get to later, but sometimes I feel I know more than I am given credit for. Programming gave me a creative outlet, somewhere that I can express myself and show others what I am really capable of. I started my first programming project outside of Minecraft in late 2024. I wanted to learn how to make a mobile app, I figured it couldn't be that hard and it would be really cool to make an app that other people could enjoy and see all of the effort that went into developing it. What is most rewarding to me often times is what people think about me; money is not a huge motivator, but performance and recognition for my efforts. I wanted to make the app free, whatever app I decided to make, but I needed an idea. I needed inspiration for what app to make.

My parents love Yahtzee,

My dad has a few apps on his phone that he can play random times, like when he is waiting for the oil change in the car or a few minutes before bed to relax, and one of his favourites is Yahtzee, or the off brand "Five Dice" since Yahtzee is copyrighted. My mom also loves Yahtzee, this was one of her dad's favourite games when she was young and it holds a special place in her heart now that he has passed. She shares a special memory when we play; we still use his original set with the same old dice and same old cup, we even have some of the old score cards with his name on it. Since I already know 3 people including myself that would enjoy a Yahtzee app, I figured this would be a great project to start on! I thought I knew the basics, I knew I didn't want to get super deep with online leaderboards or multi-player functionality, I wanted to keep it basic.
I didn't take any computer science classes in high school, I didn't have any friends that enjoyed programming either. I have been watching a Twitch streamer and YouTuber named DougDoug for a few years at this point, and he had a professional background in programming working for a large gaming company called EA. On his streams he would often times go on "five minute coding adventures" which are much longer than five minutes, sometimes taking a few hours and the entire stream will be coding. He has a wonderful way of making things exciting and fun to watch! Overstimulating the viewers senses with crazy sounds and on screen elements that even if you don't know and don't care about programming, you are still going to have a good time. I learned a lot just by watching his streams and seeing the code that he was writing, and I felt like I could do anything with one of his streams in the background. That turns out to be a lot harder than I expected...
I found a few YouTube tutorials on how to make an Android app with a program called Android Studio. This helped me get the ground work for my app, and learning how to design the front end of my code as it is very different than standard HTML. With the help of ChatGPT that I was using at the time, I was able to tell it what I was looking to make and have it explain to me how it works. ChatGPT would write the code, then explain to me what it was and how I can write it in the future and what makes it work. Here is an example of part of the code that allows the dice to "roll";

private fun rollDice() {
    val dice = listOf(dice1, dice2, dice3, dice4, dice5)

    dice.forEachIndexed { index, diceView ->
        if (!lockedDice[index]) {
            val value = (1..6).random()
            currentDiceValues[index] = value
            diceView.setImageResource(getDiceResource(value))
        }
    }
}

I have a picture of each dice face, from 1-6, saved in the code. This function, using (1..6).random(), will randomly choose a number from 1-6 and choose the dice face that corresponds to that number from the array. The actual function is a lot more complicated than this, I have the dice "spin" by rolling through very quickly scrolling between all of the images, giving the feel that the dice is rolling until it eventually stops on the randomly selected number.
All of this would not have been possible for me to create if it wasn't for the help of AI. I know what needs to be written to get the desired outcome, but I don't know how to write it. I never formally learned any coding languages, and I had never even heard of Kotlin before starting this project. The logical part of my brain, knowing how computers communicate, knows what message needs to be said to the computer, but I don't know the words to say. ChatGPT was a very important instrument when I started to learn programming, and this project took me around 4-5 months to get it where I was comfortable and happy to stop working on it. I was able to create the core game of Yahtzee, with auto calculating scores and detecting locked dice, and not letting players unselect scores so scores were locked in after each round, but also letting players unselect if they accidentally hit the wrong button. A lot of logic went into the "rules" of the game, everything needs to be defined much more clearly then when you are simply explaining to a person the rules of a game.
After I made the core game, I made a leaderboard that is stored on the device, so it is not a global leaderboard but rather a per-device leaderboard to flex high scores with friends on your same device. I made a setting page where you can customise the colours of the app and the text and dice size for if you can't read smaller text. I also created another version of Yahtzee where you can bank unused rolls and use them as extra rolls on your next turn, giving the user a better chance at getting Yahtzee towards the end of the game. I am very proud of how this app turned out, and I finished it just in time to give to my Dad as a Christmas present.

Present day,

After making this mobile app, I took a break from programming for a while and focused on other hobbies. I got the job I am working currently which is in the front office at a Marriott hotel, and the property management system they use looks pretty old; it looks like it is from the 80's and you are programming or hacking into the Pentagon. It's old and some people get very confused and it's hard to learn, but I love it. I never want it to leave! I feel very comfortable with it, and it reminded me of what programming was like and how much I enjoy it. There are reports you can create to do various things, but creating the reports can be difficult if you are not familiar with operators and terminology. After I worked on this system for a few months I decided I want to work on programming again, but this time I want to get into web development. I've always thought it would be cool to make a website, but again just like Yahtzee I lacked inspiration of what to make. I tend to be a workaholic, which I admit is not my best trait, but I am passionate and inspired to make a website that would greatly improve my company and their systems! I started working on this website and I am still developing this as I am writing this, but I was shocked with how easy creating website is with JavaScript and React front end! I will not be sharing anything about the website I am making to pitch to my company and I am keeping that between me and my work, but I can share about the website you are on now, KennDoesDev.com.

The website I mentioned above is just running on local host, and I don't plan on publishing it personally, but I did want to make a public website that I can share with people! Introducing, KennDoesDev! This stands for Kennedy Does Development, and this is where I will be publishing all of my future projects. Right now I have 3 published: a notebook, an AI email assistant, and this blog you are reading now. Let's go into a little more detail about each of these projects. I will go in depth about how they work in later blogs, but for now I can at least share what they are and what they do!

Notebook

I really like Google products, like Google slides, sheets, docs, etc. The only thing I don't like about Google docs and Word is there are not really good organization tools, especially on Mobile. I also figured a notebook is something that would be very easy and minimal to create, and this would be an incredible learning opportunity for pushing a coding project into a real website. My website is being run on Vercel servers, which pull my code from a GitHub repository, and for notebook I am using Google Firebase for authentication and as a database. Firebase is a great resource, I am still learning about all of its features, but it allows people to sign in to my project using their Google account and creates a database for each user automatically! I tried working with SQLite but man is it confusing. I am also seeing how Google authentication is very secure, as I cannot see anyone's information except their email address; I can't even access their database so their information is completely secure. I now feel more safe when logging into some other website using my Google account because I can directly see what the owner sees.
This notebook web app was very simple to make, you can create folders or pages, and you can even create locked pages! I am saving my passwords on one of the locked pages in my account, and the beauty of this is it is a website so I can access this from anywhere! I don't need to transfer anything from my phone to a new phone like passwords, it all can be accessed from a computer or anywhere. I'm very happy with how this project turned out, and making the notebook itself only took maybe 2 hours, most of the time was spent learning how to use Vercel, SquareSpace Domains, and Google Firebase.

AI Email Assistant

So many people use ChatGPT to write their emails for them. I don't really like this. I wish people were hand writing more emails and putting more thought into their messages, but I also understand time is money and when you have to write tens or hundreds of emails each day it is very helpful to have someone (or something) that can help you speed up that process. Now opening a tab on your browser and going to ChatGPT is not the best way to do this in my opinion. I have been the recipient of countless AI written emails and it just makes me sad, because it's blatant that the person emailing me didn't even take the time to proofread or edit the response to make it sound more human. There's no personality involved, and I don't like to see random bolded words or bullet points in an email that doesn't need it. That being said, I also understand that AI is an incredible resource, and people should use it to save time where they can and improve their productivity. I want to create a tool that everyone can use that specialises in helping write better emails easier, and will generate better and more human sounding responses.
Introducing, "Daniel's AI Email Assistant!"
Using Claude, the better performing and more ethical company, I trained one of their models and gave it a bunch of rules so it will generate the best email possible. This also will follow any company guidelines and can be adjusted to fit specific companies if requested. I prioritised making this strictly an email writing AI, so you cannot look up anything profane or random things on the internet. I have 2 boxes on the main page: one for composing an email, which is designed to email someone for the first time regarding whatever the user says, and the other is for replying to an email. The reply section has 2 boxes, one that I designed so you can copy an email or email chain and paste it to give the AI context, then the other box s for what you want to reply. You can say something simple like "answer by saying yes I think that's a great idea, let's order more credit card readers but give me a scope of different ones with price ranges." By saying this simple part, the AI will review the context and make a reply for the specific person you are replying to and it will fill in the blanks you may have left that need answering. It truly is a wonderful tool that I will personally be using a lot, and hopefully other people can get some use out of it as well.

I feel bad when I drag people into conversation with me, because they probably don't care at all about what I am saying but they don't want to hurt my feelings. I really just want to be heard, I want to be acknowledged, I want to be recognised for my success and not be downplayed as a goofball that doesn't know what he's talking about. This way I can voice my passions and voice my opinions, and people don't have to listen to me ramble! Speaking about rambling, I am horrible with words and speaking to people. I can never get my point across. What I like about typing is I can just think and put my thoughts into words without messing it up. I can convey the message I have in my head and people can read a coherent thought instead of countless rabbit trails. You know what's the best part? Nobody has to care. Nobody is obligated to listen to me or read what I have to say, but I am still able to fulfil my need to have my voice heard. My thoughts are out there, and I have at least one person reading every message I write; myself.

AI is a powerful tool,

but AI is a complicated business. The world is changing; businesses all across the world are looking for ways to incorporate AI in their products. Some companies like Meta, X, and Google are making their own LLMs that users can directly interact with, where other companies are looking for ways AI can help their workflow. Whether it's using AI to write emails, analyse data, or optimise production in a variety of areas, the workforce is experiencing a huge change; possibly larger than the invention of computers. When I talk about AI and tech I am going to come at it from different angles. I don't want to be overly optimistic, but it's not going to take over the world either. One negative we are seeing is AI and HR and the job market. For now AI is not directly replacing jobs in the sense an AI is taking orders at a restaurant, but AI is helping entry level office job staff be more productive so more can be done with a smaller team. A team of 10 with AI can now do the same job as a team of 15 or 20, so we are seeing some layoffs in various sectors, especially in the IT and programming fields. There are a lot of people looking for jobs in the job market, and applying for jobs is nowhere near the same process as 20 years ago.
Back in the day, the most important thing to get a job is confidence and an excellent resume. Now, anyone can ask AI to create a resume that is tailored to the specific job they are applying for. Everyone is coming to the table with a perfect resume, and everyone is painted as being perfect for the job; now hiring the right person becomes a nightmare because people aren't as perfect as they are painted to be.
On the other side of the card, HR companies are using AI to skim through candidates. AI is perfect for data analytics, so it just makes sense when you have dozens of candidates you can tell the machine what you are looking for specifically and let it choose a candidate for you. The problem becomes AI is applying for jobs to be reviewed by AI. There is no human involvement at all! This makes candidates applying for jobs unhappy because they are not getting hired and competition is too fierce, and employers are unhappy because for one they are not getting the perfect candidate they thought they were, and two the tool that was supposed to help them find a good candidate makes them all blend in; nobody is standing out.
The job market is in a pretty difficult place right now, and a lot of fresh college graduates are experiencing this first hand. Sometimes it can be difficult to be optimistic about the future of AI, but some leaders in the industry give some very good insights. One person I follow along is Dario Amodei, the former Vice President of Research at OpenAI and now the CEO of Anthropic, which is the producer of Claude AI. I am always encouraged reading his papers or blogs, as he is still optimistic without being overly optimistic like Sam Altman and others in the industry. He is also cares about the ethics questions that this new technology creates, which is evident with one of the moral questions the US Government posed a few weeks ago.

What should AI be given the power to do?

And more importantly, what should the government be allowed to do? This is an important question in times where it feels like the government keeps over-stretching their power, trying to squeeze other countries to show how much they need the US when in reality, we are showing other countries how important is for them to be self sufficient. AI, being the superpower it is, has been reaching out into military with companies like Palantir. The department of war is very interested in AI as it can prevent human casualties and can move the United States military to the next level when the world continues to build their militaries. It's important to stay ahead, sure, but when does it become too much? This is a question Dario Amodei proposed when the government was proposing a contract with Anthropic. From the gov's perspective? We are already using Claude in different aspects as people use it personally, so it juts makes sense to continue using this resource and give Anthropic monetary incentive to keep improving their product and give the US more resources. From Dario's perspective? This is a slippery slope, given what tasks AI is specialised for. Any country's government would love to keep tabs on all of their citizens, but for a country with 342 million people it's not realistic to review all of the data from every camera across the country. AI is perfect for data analytics, so a task that seems so far out of reach is now obtainable within honestly a few months. While the government has access to all traffic cameras and ring doorbell camera footage since 2022 and domestic surveillance technically isn't against the constitution, Dario believes it is unethical and violates citizens privacy. He created a hard rule, explaining if the government wants to set up a contract to use Claude they must report what they are using it for and cannot use it to "spy" on Americans.
The second hard rule that he set is relating to war. War is constantly changing, as we saw in the Ukraine war and now with this war in Iran, drones play a huge role as it causes a lot of destruction with minimal casualties on the offensive side. AI can play a huge role in this as well, and Anthropic understands this, but there has to be limits. They said if the government wants to use Claude to fly drones equipped with guns in the military, there must be a human behind the wheel to push the button to shoot. A human must be the one that chooses to take another human life. Death is a part of war, but a robot cannot be the one who chooses to take a life. This raises massive ethical concerns; I'm sure I don't need to explain everything that's wrong with this but briefly one thing is what if the wrong person get's killed?
With these two rules that Anthropic was not willing to back down from, the United States government said they would not be continuing any business with Claude and gov employees are no longer allowed to use any of their services. This was the perfect chance for other AI companies to stand up to a global superpower. This was the time where the people can push back and not just the opposing political party. Sam Altman said he agrees with the statements, however we see Open AI has signed a government contract. While not confirmed, ChatGPT very well could be killing people in wars as I am writing this blog. That is a truly scary thought. I had a paid membership to ChatGPT that I have since cancelled, and many people are joining the movement QuitGPT to boycott the most popular AI company in the western world. Since quitting, I have seen Claude is literally leagues ahead of its competitors, especially with my programming projects. In using it with my work in hospitality, I have seen claude is great with creating Excel sheets and PowerPoint presentations. It is incredibly helpful and it doesn't glaze you like other AI models. I encourage everyone to make the switch, you will see everything you use AI for will improve.
I am very optimistic about AI. I don't want what I said above to make you think I am an AI hater, but rather I want to express the bad while highlighting the good. In future blogs I will give more news in the AI space, as for now we are just watching some decisions unfold.

Conclusion

  2. One news topic, typically something related to business or tech but possibly some politics

Whether you are learning about news, learning about me and what makes my brain tick and what makes me who I am, learning Chinese along side me, or learning about God, I hope people can enjoy my content and I hope I can add something meaningful to your weekly routine.
